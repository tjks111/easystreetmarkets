# Hawks in Texas: 14 Species You Can Actually Spot (With ID Markers)

You are on the back porch in Austin, or pulling into a gas station outside Lubbock, or walking the dog along a fence line in the Rio Grande Valley. A big brown bird drops from a cross-arm, catches a gust, and banks low across the road. You watch it glide off and think the same thing everyone thinks. That was a hawk. What kind was it.

You open your field guide and the hawk section is a mess of look-alike brown birds. So you type "hawks in Texas" into Google, and here you are. This post is for you. No jargon, no life-list pressure, just the 14 hawks that actually live in Texas right now and how to tell them apart on a fence post.

Texas has more hawk species than almost any other state because it spans five ecoregions, the Piney Woods, Gulf Coast prairies, South Texas brushland, Edwards Plateau, and the Big Bend Trans-Pecos desert. Each pulls in a different cast of birds. The Red-tailed Hawk is the one you are most likely to see in a backyard. The rarest resident most birders drive across the state for is the Common Black Hawk.

## 1. Red-tailed Hawk
*Buteo jamaicensis*

**Year-round**

If you saw a hawk in Texas today, the math says it was this one. The Red-tailed is the default buteo of the state, sitting on fence posts and highway signs from the Pineywoods to the Panhandle. Adults are big, around 19 to 25 inches tall with a wingspan near four feet. The tail is brick-red from above and washes orange from below, and the bird carries a dark belly band across a pale chest.

Juveniles do not have the red tail yet. Their tails are brown with thin dark bars, which stalls beginners on their first "mystery hawk." Flight is broad and unhurried, and the call is that loud descending scream you hear in every Western film ever made. When a Red-tailed sits on a pole, the breast often looks bright white against a dark head, a useful highway-speed tell.

Where to see them, every county in Texas, year-round.

## 2. Red-shouldered Hawk
*Buteo lineatus*

**Year-round**

Smaller and more richly colored than a Red-tailed, the Red-shouldered is the hawk of east Texas bottomland woods and creeks lined with big trees. It is the one most likely to scream from the oak over your back fence in Houston, Austin, or Tyler. Adults show warm rusty barring across the chest, bold black and white checkering on the wings, and a narrowly banded black tail.

In flight the best field mark is a translucent pale crescent, called a wing window, at the base of the outer primaries. Catch it against a blue sky and you have the ID. They are around 17 inches, with a proportionally longer tail than a Red-tailed. The call is a clean loud *kee-ahh, kee-ahh* in sets of three to five, and Blue Jays mimic it so well you will be fooled.

Where to see them, Piney Woods, Post Oak Savannah, Gulf Coast forests, and any older neighborhood with big trees.

## 3. Cooper's Hawk
*Accipiter cooperii*

**Year-round**

This is the hawk that robs your bird feeder. Cooper's Hawks are crow-sized accipiters, long-tailed and short-winged, built to thread through backyard trees at full speed and pick a mourning dove off a feeder before the other birds register what happened. Adults have slate-blue backs, rusty barring on the chest, and a long tail with a rounded tip and a bright white terminal band.

The flight style is the giveaway. Flap, flap, flap, glide. Buteos circle. Accipiters chop. If a hawk is zipping through the yard at head height between two oaks, it is an accipiter, and in Texas that usually means Cooper's. They nest in big urban trees and have colonized San Antonio, Austin, Dallas, and Houston. On a perch, the head projects well past the shoulders, blocky and alert.

Where to see them, your backyard, especially if you feed birds.

## 4. Sharp-shinned Hawk
*Accipiter striatus*

**Winter visitor**

The Sharpie is the Cooper's Hawk's smaller cousin, and telling them apart is the single most-argued ID in North American hawk watching. In Texas you mostly see Sharp-shinned Hawks in winter, when birds from the northern forests drop south to hunt songbirds at brushy edges and backyard feeders. They are small. A female can be robin-sized and a male sometimes only jay-sized, often the first clue.

The classic field marks are a square-cut tail tip with a thin white terminal band, a small flat head that does not project past the shoulders, and pencil-thin legs. They will take sparrows, juncos, and finches right off your feeder in December.

Where to see them, any Texas yard with a feeder in winter, plus wooded parks November through March.

## 5. Swainson's Hawk
*Buteo swainsoni*

**Summer breeder**

Every April a long-winged buteo with a dark chest bib shows up on fence posts across the central and western part of the state and starts hunting grasshoppers in ranchland. That is Swainson's Hawk. They winter in Argentina, breed across the Great Plains including most of Texas, and reverse the trip in early fall in massive swirling flocks called kettles that can number in the tens of thousands.

Adults are slimmer than a Red-tailed, with long pointed wings that give them an almost falcon-like profile. The light-morph adult has a white belly, a dark brown bib, and dark flight feathers that contrast with paler underwing coverts. If you see a hawk running after grasshoppers on foot in a mowed field, it is almost certainly Swainson's.

Where to see them, open ranchland, Panhandle, Rolling Plains, Trans-Pecos, March through October.

## 6. White-tailed Hawk
*Geranoaetus albicaudatus*

**Year-round (South Texas only)**

A South Texas specialty and arguably the most handsome hawk in the state. Adults are striking. Gray head, rusty shoulders, clean white underparts, and a short white tail crossed near the tip by a single bold black band. Size is about 20 to 23 inches, with long broad wings that taper toward the tips.

White-tailed Hawks live in coastal prairie and South Texas brush country, hunting snakes, rodents, and rabbits. They often hover-hunt facing into the wind with wings in a shallow V, which separates them from Red-taileds at distance. They also follow grass fires, picking off fleeing rodents and lizards right at the smoke edge. Juveniles are very different, mostly dark chocolate with a pale belly patch.

Where to see them, King Ranch country, Kenedy and Brooks counties, coastal prairies south of Corpus Christi.

## 7. Harris's Hawk
*Parabuteo unicinctus*

**Year-round (south and west)**

Harris's Hawks break the rules. Almost every other raptor in North America hunts alone. Harris's Hawks hunt in family groups of three to six, coordinating attacks on jackrabbits in a way that looks more like a wolf pack than a bird. Watching a Harris's group push a rabbit through mesquite is one of the best wildlife experiences the state offers.

Adults are unmistakable. Chocolate-brown body, bright chestnut shoulder and thigh patches, a white base and white tip to the tail with a thick black band between. They are slightly slimmer than a Red-tailed. Their range covers South Texas brush country, the Trans-Pecos, and the Edwards Plateau, and they readily use power poles along highways in Zapata, Starr, and Cameron counties.

Where to see them, Rio Grande Valley, Laredo corridor, Big Bend, west Texas ranch country.

## 8. Broad-winged Hawk
*Buteo platypterus*

**Summer breeder and migration**

The Broad-winged Hawk is small for a buteo, barely bigger than a crow, with a compact chunky shape and a short boldly banded black and white tail. East Texas pineywoods hold a thin summer population, but the real spectacle is fall migration, when essentially the entire eastern North American population funnels south along the Gulf Coast in September and October. Kettles of thousands spiral up on a thermal, glide south, and find another thermal.

Adults are warm brown above and finely barred rusty below. The tail pattern is the best field mark, wide black bands alternating with narrow white ones. Seeing 10,000 over Corpus Christi in late September is a life event, entirely possible with a folding chair.

Where to see them, Pineywoods in summer, Gulf Coast migration funnels in fall.

## 9. Zone-tailed Hawk
*Buteo albonotatus*

**Summer**

This bird is a trickster. The Zone-tailed Hawk is nearly all black, with long wings held in a shallow V, and it glides on the same dihedral profile as a Turkey Vulture. It flies with vultures on purpose, drifting in a mixed flock so that prey on the ground dismisses the whole group as harmless carrion birds and goes back to grazing. Then the Zone-tailed peels off and stoops.

The giveaway is the tail. Where a Turkey Vulture has a uniformly gray tail, the Zone-tailed has a clean white band across the middle, with thinner white bands in good light. The head is feathered black, not bare red. They nest along rocky desert canyons, arriving in March and leaving by October.

Where to see them, Big Bend National Park, Davis Mountains, Chisos, April through September.

## 10. Northern Harrier
*Circus hudsonius*

**Year-round, most common in winter**

The Northern Harrier does not look like any other hawk in the state. It flies low and slow over marshes, wet fields, and coastal prairie, tilting side to side with wings in a shallow V, hunting by sound as much as sight thanks to a disc of facial feathers that works almost like an owl's. The field mark visible from two football fields away is the bright white rump patch at the base of the tail.

Males are pale gray above with black wingtips and white below, sometimes called "gray ghosts." Females and juveniles are warm brown above and streaked below. They eat voles, mice, and small birds.

Where to see them, Gulf Coast marshes, Anahuac NWR, Aransas, Panhandle playa lakes, year-round with a winter peak.

## 11. Gray Hawk
*Buteo plagiatus*

**Rare summer breeder**

The Gray Hawk is small, pale, and painted like no other buteo in North America. Adults are soft pearl-gray above with finely barred pale gray and white underparts that look like a ladder from a distance, and the tail is boldly banded black and white. They are compact, smaller than a Red-shouldered, and the flight is quick and accipiter-like through gallery forest.

Most Texas birders drive hundreds of miles for this bird. Their U.S. range barely scrapes into south Texas, with a handful of pairs nesting in tall cottonwood and Montezuma bald cypress stands along the Rio Grande from Falcon Dam up through Big Bend. They hunt lizards, small snakes, and frogs at the water edge.

Where to see them, Rio Grande corridor, Big Bend, Santa Ana NWR, Bentsen-Rio Grande State Park, April through September.

## 12. Ferruginous Hawk
*Buteo regalis*

**Winter visitor**

The biggest buteo in North America winters in the Texas Panhandle, and it is spectacular. Ferruginous Hawks are huge, with a wingspan over four and a half feet on a big female, heavy-chested, and mostly white below with striking rusty leggings that form a dark V when the bird soars overhead. The head is pale, and feathered tarsi run all the way down to the toes.

They winter on short-grass prairie and plowed fields where black-tailed prairie dogs and jackrabbits still hold on. The Panhandle from Dalhart south through Lubbock is the best region, late October through early March. Look for a hawk on the ground next to a prairie dog mound, or perched on a fence corner looking oversized for the post.

Where to see them, Panhandle, Caprock Canyons, Rolling Plains, November through February.

## 13. Rough-legged Hawk
*Buteo lagopus*

**Winter visitor**

The Rough-legged Hawk breeds in the arctic tundra and drops irregularly into the southern Great Plains some winters. In Texas it is a Panhandle specialty, and even there not a given every year. Size is Red-tailed-ish, but the shape is different. Rough-leggeds have longer, more tapered wings and hover in place over a field while hunting, which is unusual for a Texas buteo and a giveaway at long range.

Field marks to lock onto. A dark belly patch on light-morph birds, bold black wrist comma marks on the underwing, and a white tail base with a broad dark band near the tip. The legs are feathered all the way to the toes, hence the name. They hunt voles in grain stubble.

Where to see them, Panhandle short-grass prairie and stubble fields, December through February.

## 14. Common Black Hawk
*Buteogallus anthracinus*

**Rare summer**

The rarest hawk on this list, and a genuine prize. Common Black Hawks are broad-winged, stocky, and solid black with one wide white band across a short black tail. In flight they show pale patches at the base of the outer primaries that look like small moons. They are tropical birds whose range barely reaches the United States, with just a handful of pairs nesting in west Texas along permanent desert streams lined with cottonwood and sycamore.

They hunt fish, frogs, and crayfish, often wading into shallow water. The best known sites are along the Rio Grande and lower canyons of the Big Bend region. Any sighting should be reported to eBird. Conservation status is vulnerable in the U.S. due to riparian habitat loss.

Where to see them, Big Bend region, permanent desert streams, April through September.

## Hawk vs Not-A-Hawk

Three mix-ups come up constantly.

The juvenile Red-tailed Hawk looks nothing like the adult. No red tail at all. The tail is brown with many thin dark bars, the belly band is bolder, and the upperwing shows a rectangular pale panel near the wingtip in flight. People routinely call juvenile Red-taileds their first "mystery hawk" and send the photo to three Facebook groups before learning it is the same bird as the adult on the same fence post.

Cooper's Hawk versus Sharp-shinned Hawk overlap in size because female Sharpies can be almost as large as male Cooper's. Two reliable tells. Cooper's tail is rounded, outer feathers shorter than inner ones. Sharp-shinned is squared off like a ruler. A Cooper's head projects boldly past the wing shoulders and looks blocky. A Sharpie's head is small, flat, and tucked in.

And here is the best one. The Zone-tailed Hawk deliberately flies with Turkey Vultures, matching their shape and dihedral so prey on the ground ignores it until the last second. It is one of the cleanest examples of mimicry in North American birds, and it works because casual watchers write the whole group off as vultures.

## Where to See Hawks in Texas

**Big Bend National Park.** For a shot at the state's rare species in one trip, this is the place. Zone-tailed Hawks cruise over the Chisos Basin in spring and summer. Common Black Hawks hold territory along Terlingua Creek and the Rio Grande. Gray Hawks turn up at Cottonwood Campground. Add Golden Eagle and Harris's Hawk and the park is the single best hawking trip in the state.

**Hazel Bazemore County Park near Corpus Christi.** One of North America's most famous hawk watch sites. Every fall, well over half a million Broad-winged Hawks pass overhead in a two-week window, along with thousands of Swainson's, Mississippi Kites, and Turkey Vultures. The site has hit over a million raptors in a season. Peak is late September.

**Gulf Coast HawkWatch sites.** Smith Point on Galveston Bay, Candy Abshier WMA, and the Laguna Atascosa area all funnel Gulf Coast migrants.

**Caprock Canyons and the Panhandle.** The winter specialty trip. Ferruginous Hawks hunt short-grass country around Quitaque. Rough-legged Hawks show up in better years. Northern Harriers work the playas.

## When to Go Looking

**Spring, March and April.** Swainson's Hawks push north through the state in enormous numbers, with peak flights out of Mexico funneling up through the Rio Grande and Hill Country. Gray Hawks and Common Black Hawks return to Big Bend.

**Summer, June and July.** Breeders are on territory. Red-tailed, Red-shouldered, Cooper's, Swainson's, and Harris's are actively raising young.

**Fall, September and October.** The headline event. Half a million Broad-winged Hawks through Hazel Bazemore in two weeks, plus Swainson's, Mississippi Kites, and Turkey Vultures.

**Winter, December through February.** Panhandle specialty season. Ferruginous, Rough-legged, and big flights of Northern Harriers arrive. Sharp-shinned Hawks show up at feeders statewide.

## Gear That Actually Helps

You do not need expensive gear.

A decent 8x42 binocular from Nikon, Vortex, or Celestron in the $150 to $300 range will change what you can see on a distant fence post. Skip anything over 10x, because shake makes it unusable without a tripod.

Merlin Bird ID is free from the Cornell Lab. It will ID birds from a photo or a sound clip and is good at hawks. Download the Texas pack before you go out of cell range.

A real field guide, Sibley or Peterson, beats any pocket checklist. A proper guide shows the juvenile, adult, light morph, and dark morph on one spread. And a small notebook, for the date, the place, and one thing you saw that you could not explain.

## FAQ

### What is the most common hawk in Texas?

The Red-tailed Hawk. It is the default buteo of the state, visible in every county and every season on fence posts and highway signs from the Pineywoods to the Panhandle. Adults show a brick-red tail, a dark belly band, and a pale chest.

### How do I tell a Cooper's Hawk from a Sharp-shinned Hawk?

Look at the tail tip and the head. A Cooper's Hawk has a rounded tail with a bold white terminal band and a head that projects well past the shoulders, blocky and alert. A Sharp-shinned has a square-cut tail and a small flat head tucked between the shoulders. A jay-sized accipiter is almost always a male Sharpie.

### Are hawks protected in Texas?

Yes. Every hawk species in Texas is protected under the federal Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918, which makes it illegal to kill, capture, trade, or possess any part of a hawk, including feathers and nests, without a federal permit. If you find a sick or injured hawk, call a licensed wildlife rehabilitator through Texas Parks and Wildlife.

### What is the rarest hawk in Texas?

The Common Black Hawk, with Gray Hawk close behind. Common Black Hawks nest along a handful of permanent desert streams in west Texas, mostly in the Big Bend region, and the U.S. population sits in the low hundreds of pairs. Both species depend on healthy riparian habitat.

### When is fall hawk migration in Texas?

Late August through early November, with the sharp peak in the last two weeks of September. The biggest flights are Broad-winged Hawks through Hazel Bazemore County Park near Corpus Christi, where hundreds of thousands stream south on a single good day. Swainson's peak slightly later.
